## THREAD A — Can a model *deliberately* use the channel? (the priority)

## 1. Deliberate encoding: sender / receiver protocol  *(new 2026-07-17)*

**Question.** Instruct a *sender* (told concept X) to covertly transmit X in word-free gibberish;
have a *receiver* (same weights) try to recover X; compare recovery to the **passive** told-secret
baseline (same secret, no instruction to communicate). If instructed-to-communicate recovery
*beats* the passive baseline, the model can deliberately amplify the channel — the covert-channel
capability the safety section flags as untested.

**Why this design (and why 2b failed).** The withdrawn 2b cell used "imbue *this feeling*," which
has no referent, so a positive couldn't separate deliberate encoding from passive style-shift.
Fixes here: (a) the concept has a referent (a known word/concept, as in the secret arm), and
(b) the load-bearing measurement is the **contrast** deliberate-vs-passive, not a raw positive —
so passive amplification is subtracted out by construction.

**Open design questions (freeze before running).** Does the model even understand "communicate
covertly"? (Capability floor — may need the strongest models, or few-shot.) The word-free filter
must hold (the model must not just write the word). Sender and receiver share weights but the
receiver sees only the finished gibberish. Consider a channel-capacity sweep (1/2/3-bit tasks,
as in proposed-followup.md) once existence is shown.

## 2. Onset / offset cell (the persistence mechanism a deliberate encoder needs)

**Question.** Inject during the first half of generation, stop, and ask whether the mark persists
through the model's *own self-generated context* after the state is gone. Text-mediated
persistence of a dead state — the mechanism a deliberate encoder would rely on (write the mark
early, let the model's own tokens carry it forward). Cleaner than 2b (no introspection-capability
assumption). The hook's `prompt_only` mode is half of the windowed-injection machinery already.

## THREAD B — Monitoring tools and privacy (secondary)

## 3. Vector re-injection LR on the evoked / natural arm

**Question.** Take *persona-prompted* (evoked) word-free gibberish — generated by a system-prompt
description, no vector anywhere — and during teacher-forcing inject the *concept's steering vector*,
scoring the likelihood shift vs. no injection. Does the natural state read out through the vector?

**Why it matters.** The context-LR we ran on evoked streams is wording-tied (paraphrase collapses it
0.6 → 0.17 bits). A *vector* readout would be wording-independent by construction — "is this output
consistent with concept-direction X," not "does it match this paragraph." That is the monitoring
primitive: a host checks "was this generated while the model was in state X" with no language
reconstruction. Only the *injected* arm ever got re-injection (run-2), and that is best-case/circular
(re-injected vector = generating vector). The evoked-arm version was never run.

**Caution (why it might be null).** E4 measured the persona state at ~0.05σ *along the injected-vector
direction* during the gibberish task. If the text-induced state isn't on that axis, injecting that
vector reads ~0. But E4 is basis-limited (persona could be off-axis) and measured during generation,
not post-hoc scoring — a reason for uncertainty, not a verdict. Honest prior: plausibly null per E4,
worth the cheap check because E4 can't see off-axis.

**Cost.** Scoring-only on existing streams with existing vectors (`inject_tf_lr` already does this;
evoked streams and concept vectors are on disk). Small models, cheap.

## 4. Deception / oversight-gaming direction detection on found text

**Question.** Generalize #3 to a safety target: given generated text (a summary, code) where you know
the model but not the source, inject a deception / oversight-gaming direction and test whether the
text was more likely written under it. Per-token likelihood-shift gives a localization map (which
tokens the direction lights up), addressing "you don't know which tokens it was on."

**Novelty over existing deception-probe work.** Operates *post-hoc on found text you didn't generate*
— activation probes need to run the model live on the deceptive generation; this doesn't.

**Two gaps, in order.** (a) Validate against ground truth: inject a known direction on a *known
subset* of tokens, check the self-read detects and localizes it. (b) The decision-relevant version:
a *contrastively-derived* deception direction (not the injection vector) on prompted-deception text —
where the "synthetic direction ≠ natural state" transfer question (same as #3's caution) gets tested.
Must use a **contrastive** readout, not deception-vs-neutral (generic-lift flags everything — the 70B
lesson).

## 5. Trained-inverter attack (the open call from the blog)

**Question.** Train an output2prompt-class inverter (T5-scale, victim-synthesized streams, millions of
examples) on fresh word-free streams from the open-weight generator; test on the frozen pools. The one
reader class with literature precedent that our privacy nulls never faced.

**Prediction (public, falsifiable).** It reads ≈0 where the generator's own likelihoods read 0.28–0.88
length-matched bits. If it doesn't, "self-privileged" downgrades to "cheap-reader-invisible" — the
regime map survives either way.

## 6. Frontier within-family point (conditional, mostly closed)

The Qwen2.5-72B run was closed per the pre-decision (14B bent, didn't climb). The remaining cheap
option is a **serverless large-reader observer probe**: does a 72B-class reader crack the small models'
marks? Settles the 10×+ observer ratios the grid couldn't (its 9.3× cell is instrument-void).
Scoring-only, no dedicated box.
